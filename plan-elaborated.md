Let me walk through everything from scratch — not dumbed down, but with the *reasoning* explained so you understand *why* each piece exists, not just *what* it is.

The core idea first :-

Why does this system exist in two tiers?
BSE has 5000 stocks. You cannot analyse all 5000 deeply every few minutes — that would require enormous computing power and most of it would be wasted on stocks that aren't worth trading today. So the system is split: Tier 1 runs quietly in the background all day, constantly narrowing 5000 stocks down to 1–3 genuinely good candidates. Tier 2 only kicks in when the user says "start trading" — and by that point, it already knows which stock to focus on. The expensive, deep analysis only happens on the best candidate.
Think of Tier 1 as a funnel that's always running, and Tier 2 as a specialist who only looks at what the funnel hands them.

Tier 1 — the four stages explained :-
1) Universe sanitation — "is this stock safe to even touch?"
Before any analysis, you need to throw out stocks that could hurt you in ways that have nothing to do with price movement. ASM and GSM are government watchlists — BSE publishes them daily — listing stocks suspected of manipulation or very unusual trading. These stocks can look like they're moving well but then suddenly freeze or collapse for regulatory reasons. If a company has an announcement today (paying dividends, splitting shares), its price behaves abnormally and your models — which were trained on normal days — won't apply. Stocks below ₹50 or above ₹5000 are excluded because they're either penny stocks with unpredictable behavior or so expensive that you can't buy enough quantity to make meaningful profit within a normal account size.
This stage isn't about finding good stocks. It's about removing dangerous ones. You go from ~5000 to ~250.

2) Hard liquidity gate — "can I actually get in and out without losing money on the trade itself?"
Liquidity means: how much money is being traded in this stock right now. A stock with low liquidity means very few buyers and sellers, so when you want to buy, there's nobody to sell to you at a fair price — you end up paying more than you should. When you want to exit, nobody wants to buy from you at a fair price, so you sell for less. This gap between what you pay to enter and what you receive to exit is called the "spread", and it's a guaranteed loss on every trade. ADV (Average Daily Value) of ₹10 Crore means the stock trades at least ₹10 Crore per day on average — enough that your order won't move the price just by existing. ATR% measures how much the stock typically moves in a day as a percentage. If a stock only moves 0.5% on average, but your round-trip trading cost is 0.25%, you've already given away half your potential profit before anything else happens.
The liquidity gate exists because a trade can be perfectly correct in direction but still lose money if the stock is too thinly traded to get a fair price.

3) Momentum ignition scan — "is something actually happening in this stock right now?"
RVOL (Relative Volume) compares today's trading volume at this hour to the average volume at this same hour over the last 10 days. If a stock normally trades 50,000 shares between 10:00–10:30 AM but today it's trading 120,000 shares in that window, the RVOL is 2.4x. That spike usually means institutional investors — large funds, banks — are moving money into or out of this stock. Those are the players whose orders actually move prices. VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) is the average price of the stock weighted by how much was traded at each price. It's where most of the day's business has happened. A stock trading above its VWAP and rising means buyers are in control and willing to pay more. The opening range is the high and low set in the first 15 minutes of trading — when a stock breaks above that range later in the day with high volume, it's usually a sign of genuine momentum beginning.
You're not looking for stocks that moved. You're looking for stocks that are beginning to move with institutional participation behind them. That combination has follow-through.
The RVOL + VWAP combination and the "opening range expansion" concept came from Response 2, which described these as the signals for "momentum ignition." The volume acceleration derivative — looking at whether volume is growing at an increasing rate rather than just being high — is a new addition not in any of the three responses.

4) Regime detection — "what kind of market day is today?"
This is the most important stage and the one most systems skip entirely. Not all market days behave the same way. On a trending day, prices move steadily in one direction all day — stocks that break out keep going. On a mean-reversion day, any stock that moves strongly in one direction tends to snap back to where it started — breakouts fail. On an event day (RBI rate decision, budget announcement), volatility goes completely outside historical norms and all your calibrated models become unreliable. On a choppy day, there's no consistent pattern at all. If you apply trend-following logic on a mean-reversion day, you'll lose every trade — not because your stock-picking was wrong, but because the type of day was wrong. Regime detection identifies which kind of day it is by 9:45 AM (after 30 minutes of market open), and then changes how the system scores and weights everything that follows.
The same signal means completely different things on different types of days. Regime detection is what allows the system to interpret signals in context rather than mechanically.

Tier 2 — the four agents explained :-

Technical analyst agent — "what does the chart structure say?"
This agent looks at the stock's price history across three different time scales simultaneously. The 15-minute chart tells you the overall direction of the trade — is the stock in an uptrend or downtrend over the past few hours? The 5-minute chart shows you the specific setup — has the stock pulled back to a key level and started bouncing, or is it breaking above a resistance point where lots of selling was previously happening? The 1-minute chart shows you the precise timing — right now, at this second, is the trade ready to enter? All three time scales need to agree. If the 15-minute chart says uptrend but the 1-minute is falling, you wait. This multi-timeframe approach dramatically reduces false entries compared to looking at only one timeframe.
Response 2 included this agent with examples of RSI and MACD outputs. The three-timeframe requirement (15m for direction, 5m for setup, 1m for timing) is an addition here. Response 3 mentioned "multiple timeframe analysis" in passing but didn't explain the logic.

Microstructure agent — "what is the order book actually showing?"
The order book shows all pending buy orders (bids) and sell orders (asks) sitting in the exchange, grouped by price. If there are 200,000 shares of buy orders stacked below the current price and only 30,000 shares of sell orders above it, that imbalance means buyers heavily outnumber sellers at this moment — a strong leading indicator that the price is likely to rise. But there's a subtlety: large orders appearing and then disappearing rapidly (called spoofing) are fake signals placed to mislead other traders. The agent therefore doesn't just look at the current snapshot — it watches how the order book has changed over the last 2–3 minutes. Real institutional buying shows orders being steadily filled and consumed. Fake orders appear and then vanish before they're filled.
The order book is the only genuinely leading indicator in the market — it shows intentions before they become transactions.
Response 2 had the best description of this agent, noting "institutional accumulation detected" and buy/sell imbalance. The addition here is tracking order book dynamics over time (orders appearing vs orders being filled) rather than a single snapshot reading.

Catalyst agent — "is there a reason for this move, and is it safe?"
Sometimes a stock moves because of genuine news — a good earnings result, a new contract announcement, a positive regulatory development. That kind of move often continues. But sometimes a stock moves for dangerous reasons — a court ruling pending, a regulatory investigation, a promoter selling shares. The catalyst agent checks BSE's announcement feed for any news in the last 4 hours about this specific stock. It also checks whether the stock's sector is moving in the same direction (if the whole banking sector is rising and this bank stock is rising, that's safer than a bank stock rising while all other banks fall — the latter suggests something stock-specific and unknown). The news veto works like this: if there's a headline that could explain the move but introduces binary uncertainty (the stock goes to 0 if the court rules one way, or doubles if it rules another), the system skips the trade. No technical setup, no matter how good, justifies a coin-flip risk.
Response 2 described this agent with an example about Reliance's green energy announcement. The news veto concept — rejecting trades where a headline introduces binary outcome risk — is an addition not fully articulated in any of the three responses.

Volatility agent — "where exactly should the stop loss go?"
Every stock moves randomly even when the overall trend is clear. This random movement is called "noise." If you place your stop loss too close to your entry price, the normal daily noise will trigger it even when you're fundamentally correct about the direction — you get stopped out and then watch the stock go where you predicted. ATR (Average True Range) measures how much a stock typically moves in any given period including its random noise. Placing the stop at 1.5× ATR below entry means you've given the stock enough room to breathe through its normal noise, but if it actually reverses beyond that level, you know the trade was wrong. The additional improvement here: the stop is also compared against the nearest structural support level — a price zone where the stock has previously found buyers multiple times. Ideally the stop sits just below that support level, because if buyers historically came in at ₹450 and the stock breaks below ₹450, those buyers are now wrong and the stock will likely fall further. Your stop at ₹449 captures this logic.
Response 3 described ATR-based stop placement. The combination with structural support levels — always placing the stop at whichever of the two is both tighter and structurally defensible — is an addition here.

The meta-agent and risk gate explained :-
Meta-agent (CIO) — "do all signals agree, and are they independent?"
When four experts all look at the same situation and all say "yes, do it," that's only meaningful if they arrived at that conclusion through different reasoning. If all four of them read the same newspaper article this morning and are all reacting to that article, their agreement means nothing — it's one signal, not four. The meta-agent checks this. Technical analysis says "buy" because the chart broke above resistance. Microstructure says "buy" because institutional orders are being filled. Catalyst says "buy" because there was a positive BSE announcement. Volatility says "the stop is tight enough that this is worth taking." These are four independent reasons. That is genuine confluence. Contrast with: all four say "buy" because the stock gapped up 3% at open — they're all reacting to the same gap. That is false confluence.
Response 2 described the CIO role well but only checked whether agents agreed. The signal independence check is new here.


The risk gate — "even if everything looks good, does this trade meet the minimum requirements?"
The risk gate is the last wall before a trade is placed. Even if all four agents agree, the trade is rejected if any of these conditions fail: confidence score below 72% (the system isn't sure enough), reward-to-risk ratio below 1:2 (the potential profit is less than double the potential loss), spread is too wide (execution will eat the edge), daily loss limit already hit (no more trading today regardless), more than 3 positions already open (concentration risk), two of three open positions already in the same sector (if that sector crashes, all three lose simultaneously), or the market data feed has a lag or error. The last one matters more than it sounds — making a trading decision on data that's 30 seconds stale in a fast-moving market is equivalent to driving while looking in the rearview mirror.
The risk gate exists because a system without hard rules will always find a reason to take a trade. The gate removes human discretion from the moment when greed or fear is most likely to override good judgment.

The expectancy math — why win rate is the wrong thing to track :- 

What actually creates consistent monthly profits
Most people think a good trading system wins more than it loses. This is wrong. A system that wins 45% of its trades but makes 3× as much on each winner as it loses on each loser is significantly more profitable than a system that wins 65% of the time but makes only the same amount it risks. Here's the arithmetic: if you risk ₹1000 per trade — 45 wins give you ₹3000 each = ₹1,35,000. 55 losses cost you ₹1000 each = ₹55,000. Net: ₹80,000 profit on 100 trades. The 65% win-rate system with equal wins and losses: 65 wins at ₹1000 = ₹65,000. 35 losses at ₹1000 = ₹35,000. Net: ₹30,000 profit on 100 trades. The asymmetric system makes nearly 3× more money while being "wrong" more often. This is why the system enforces a minimum 1:2 reward-to-risk on every trade.

Now let me explain the parts that weren't in the widget — the feedback loop, the drawdown rules, and the phasing — in the same way.

**The feedback loop — why the system must learn from itself**

Every trade the system takes gets saved to a database: what the champion score was, what RVOL was, what the order book imbalance was, what regime the day was classified as, and what actually happened to the trade. After a few weeks, you have a dataset of trades with their outcomes. You can then ask: of all the signals the system used, which ones actually predicted whether a trade won or lost? You'll likely find that some signals you thought mattered don't predict outcomes at all — they just looked useful in theory. And some signals you weighted lightly turn out to be the most predictive. You update the scoring weights accordingly. This is why the system gets better over time instead of decaying — markets shift, and a model that can recalibrate based on recent outcomes stays sharp while a static model gradually drifts out of tune.

**The drawdown rules — why conservative limits actually produce more money**

"Drawdown" means how much the account has fallen from its highest point. The daily kill switch at −1.5% means: if today's trading has already cost 1.5% of the account, stop for the day. The reason is that losing money makes traders psychologically desperate to recover it, which leads to taking worse trades with more risk, which leads to losing more. Markets are not more predictable just because you need to recover a loss. By cutting the day off hard, you remove the emotional spiral entirely. Similarly, if the account drops 4% below the monthly high-water mark, position size drops to half. If it reaches 6%, no more trading that month. These limits sound painful but they ensure that a bad week can never compound into a bad month, and a bad month can never compound into catastrophic account damage.

**On the claim of "never making a loss"**

Every single trade the system takes will sometimes be wrong. This is true of every trading system that has ever existed, including those run by the world's largest hedge funds with thousands of staff and billions in technology. The market is genuinely uncertain and no amount of analysis eliminates that. What the system above achieves is that over a month of trading, the wins are large enough and the losses are controlled enough that the net result is consistently positive. The goal is not zero losing trades — it is a positive account balance at the end of every month, achieved by making the losing trades small and the winning trades large. That is the realistic and achievable version of what you're building.
